# Critique — 2026-05-13

Twenty-one moves shipped in the last month. The catalogue, codex, FAQ, PDP, running folio, specimen plate, and index menu read as competent editorial-commerce craft. The bar is Awwwards SOTD this week. From that bar the site reads as **a well-skinned Next.js template wearing an editorial costume**, not Vol. III. Every section below is grounded in surface and file:line.

## Verdict

On a weak Tuesday a juror might bookmark BFS for the copy and the colophon; on a strong day they close the tab at the hero, because "Dark Matter." is the only typographic event on the page and even that event reads as set, not *typeset*.

## Top 5 weaknesses

### 1. The hero is the only display moment, and it is conservative

- `src/app/globals.css:787` — `font-size: clamp(72px, 16vw, 288px)` is a sane scale. A brand whose register *is* type should be at `min 96px, max 360px, fluid 22vw`, with an optical-size or grade axis interpolated against scroll. The type is big, not behaving.
- `globals.css:805–812` — `hero-word-2` outlines via `-webkit-text-stroke`. Every editorial Next.js demo on Awwwards 2024–2026 uses the same mechanic. No variable-axis shift on pointer, no italic-roman swap, no ligature alternates cycling. Bureau Borsche: type is *live*. BFS type is *placed*.
- `page.tsx:99–115` — `SplitText` stagger fires once on mount and never again. Re-typeset on chapter change, or a kerning shift on viewport exit, would justify the device. Currently a polished JS demo.

### 2. Motion is a vocabulary of one word: "reveal"

- `src/components/reveal.tsx:1–55` — every section wraps in `Reveal` with a 0.08–0.6s delay. Opacity 0→1 plus a transform. A juror has seen this exact component in fifty templates this month.
- No view transitions on in-page navigation, no scroll-bound page-turn between chapters, no held masthead that catalogue slides *under* (Locomotive's basic move). The chapter rail tracks visually; the navigation itself doesn't behave like a folio.
- Catalogue scrolls as a single linear column with `data-orientation` alternating even/odd (`page.tsx:224`). Symmetry pretending to be asymmetry.

### 3. The layout still respects the grid too much

- `page.tsx:202, 386, 448, 530, 587` — six near-identical `section-head` preambles (tag → title → lede). The Next.js editorial template rhythm. No spread that photographs as a printed page: no left-page-right-page asymmetry, no full-bleed plate facing a 4-column body, no marginalia in the gutter.
- Press section (`page.tsx:499–527`) is a centered grid of plain text — the most generic "as featured in." The disclaimer is sharp; the layout is Webflow.
- The publisher's-mark colophon (`page.tsx:323–383`) is the closest thing to a printed spread, sitting mid-page surrounded by stacked sections. It earns its weight; nothing around it does.

### 4. The micro-interactions only reward the cursor that already knows where to hover

- `data-cursor="link"` is pervasive; the reward is uniform — the cursor swells. Per-surface vocabulary ("Specimen", "Plate", "Read") exists but is inconsistent. The mouse never discovers a hidden affordance.
- Catalogue tags (`page.tsx:241–247`) are `aria-hidden` divs with no hover, no target. They look interactive.
- Hero spec ribbon (`page.tsx:149–164`) — `Counter` animates on mount, never on pointer. "Light absorbed · 550 nm" is the perfect place for a scrub. Currently dead text.

### 5. The voice has tells

- "Enter the catalogue", "Read the position", "Return to the catalogue" (`page.tsx:135, 143, 623`) — three imperatives in editorial dress. A real editorial object never tells you to *enter* anything; it sets a title and trusts you.
- Outro disclosure (`page.tsx:683–688`) — "Disclosure. Yes — we are aware of how the name reads…" — is the founder-transparency move from a 2021 D2C launch. It works as a joke but is legible *as that joke*, dating the brand to the era it's trying to escape.
- "plausible publications" (`page.tsx:502`) and "lifted from correspondence" (`page.tsx:541`) overuse the same arch-aside posture. One tone repeated reads as tic, not register.
